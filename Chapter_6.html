Chapter 6

Chapter Summary & Character Study
Summary

Milly meets Mrs. Stringham and asks if they can leave their current location the next morning instead of stopping there. Mrs. Stringham agrees.

Later, Milly asks Mrs. Stringham what Dr. Finch said to her in a private meeting in New York. Mrs. Stringham replies that the doctor said nothing of consequence. Milly admits she only guessed that the meeting took place. A conversation follows in which Mrs. Stringham asks if Milly is unwell. Milly says she is not in pain but makes several cryptic comments about her life and happiness. When Mrs. Stringham offers to find a doctor, Milly says she will decide on their destination at supper, and then leaves the room.

At supper, Milly announces she wants to go to London. Mrs. Stringham mentions a potential acquaintance there, Mr. Merton Densher, but Milly says he is still in America. Mrs. Stringham then speaks of her own estranged school friend, Maud Manningham (now Mrs. Lowder), who lives in London and suggests she might try to contact her. Milly encourages her to do so and then goes to bed. Alone, Mrs. Stringham gets out her portfolio and writes a letter.

Character Study

Based on the text, here is a concise character study.

### Milly Theale

  • **Presentation:** Milly is introduced as deeply preoccupied and forgetful, signs of a significant internal state that soon surfaces. She is enigmatic and speaks in evasive, almost poetic metaphors when questioned about her well-being, describing her potential ailment as an inability to "resist the bliss of what I have." This behavior perplexes and concerns her companion. She has a "constant white face" but also a "mocking light" in her expression, suggesting a complex mix of physical fragility and sharp intelligence.
  • **Situational Shift:** Milly abruptly moves from a state of passive unease to decisive action. She engineers an immediate change in their travel plans, first urging a departure and then, after a tense and indirect conversation about her health, declaring a specific destination: London. Her stated reason is a sudden, urgent desire for "people," which she connects to a sense that her time may be limited. Her feigned forgetfulness and subsequent contradictory statements about Mr. Densher suggest he is an unstated, significant motive for this shift.

  • **Presentation:** Mrs. Stringham is presented as an intensely observant, intuitive, and accommodating companion. She instantly perceives Milly's distress and agrees to her wishes without question, attempting to ease the situation with gentle humor. She is startled and frightened by Milly’s abrupt questions about the doctor, revealing a protective and deeply caring nature. While she is often "put off" and confused by Milly's cryptic manner, her primary response is always one of earnest, tender concern.
  • **Situational Shift:** Initially a responsive caretaker reacting to Milly's moods, Mrs. Stringham is spurred into a proactive role by the decision to go to London. She seizes the opportunity to propose reconnecting with a long-lost school friend, Maud Lowder. This practical suggestion is revealed to be driven by a more personal motivation: a desire for "poetic justice" after feeling socially "shunted" by Maud years ago. Milly becomes her "trophy," and Mrs. Stringham ends the scene by immediately taking action and writing a letter, shifting from follower to facilitator.
