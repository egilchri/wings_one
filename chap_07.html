Chapter VII

Chapter Summary & Character Study
Summary

Milly and her companion, Susie Stringham, are at a dinner party at Lancaster Gate, hosted by Mrs. Lowder. Milly is seated next to a gentleman, Lord Mark, and tells him she barely knows where she is. There are twenty people at the table.

Before dinner, Mrs. Lowder's niece, Kate Croy, introduced Lord Mark to Milly, and he escorted her to the table. During the meal, Milly observes Kate across the table, and their eyes meet.

Milly and Lord Mark have a long conversation. He tells her that London society has no real "sets," calls Mrs. Lowder an "extraordinary woman," and says Kate is "tremendously... good-looking." Milly recounts to him that Mrs. Lowder invited them to dine soon after their arrival and visited their hotel with Kate.

Lord Mark tells Milly that she is a social success and is being "jumped at." He asks what Mrs. Lowder knows about her, and says he will observe what Mrs. Lowder "does with" her. When Milly asks what their hostess knows about him, he says "Nothing," and that Milly is now Mrs. Lowder's best new acquisition. Milly asks what he meant by Mrs. Lowder doing something with her, and he replies that she will "get her money back" because "nobody here, you know, does anything for nothing."

Milly calls Mrs. Lowder an idealist. She tells Lord Mark that he and the others are familiar with everything but have no imagination. She then asks him what Kate has to gain from being welcoming to her. As she asks, Kate looks over at them. Lord Mark replies that Kate gains Milly’s acquaintance. Milly suggests Kate's motive is pity. Lord Mark says he is not sorry for Milly at all. He admits he does not understand Kate and asks Milly to tell him about her when she knows more. Milly concludes that Kate must be difficult, while she herself is easy.

Based on the text, here is a concise character study:

  • *Milly Theale**
    • Milly is presented as a highly sensitive and observant American, newly thrust into a sophisticated English social scene. Her situation has shifted with dizzying speed from that of a tourist to the center of attention at a formal dinner, leaving her feeling disoriented yet exhilarated. She is acutely conscious of being evaluated, analyzing everyone around her while aware that she herself is being "jumped at." She is prone to seeing her situation in romantic terms ("fairy-tale") but is also immediately perceptive of complex social dynamics and "possibly sinister motive."
  • *Susie (Mrs. Stringham)**
    • Milly's companion, Susie, is presented through Milly's eyes as a triumphant "fairy godmother." Her situation has shifted from being a traveling friend to the successful orchestrator of Milly's social debut. She now "glitters" with a sense of success, content with the results of her efforts.
  • *Lord Mark**
    • Lord Mark is Milly's dinner partner and is presented as a collection of contradictions: he looks both old and young, seems both "intellectual" and "frivolous," and acts both clever and indifferent. As a member of the aristocracy, he is world-weary and speaks cynically of London society, yet he is also perceptive. He immediately categorizes Milly as a predictable American "type" but also declares her a "success," engaging her in a probing, almost intimate conversation.
  • *Kate Croy**
    • Referred to as "the handsome girl," Kate is presented primarily through her striking beauty and "eminence." She is positioned as a figure of great interest and mystery. Though she appears "charming" and "frankly conscious" of friendship, Milly senses she is a complex individual whom even insiders like Lord Mark cannot "make out." Her situation is that of an established social figure who immediately fascinates the newcomer, Milly.
  • *Mrs. Lowder**
The hostess is presented as a powerful and substantial figure, described as "massive" and a "great reality." Her situation has shifted from being a distant acquaintance of Susie's to the formidable architect of the evening. She is perceived by Milly as an "idealist," but there are immediate hints that she is a pragmatic social operator who is using Lord Mark to assess Milly's "value."

They were already dining, she and her friend, at Lancaster Gate, and surrounded, as it seemed to her, with every English accessory; though her consciousness of Mrs. Lowder's existence, and still more of her remarkable identity, had been of so recent and so sudden a birth. Susie, as she was apt to call her companion for a lighter change, had only had to wave a neat little wand for the fairy-tale to begin at once; in consequence of which Susie now glittered—for, with Mrs. Stringham's new sense of success, it came to that—in the character of a fairy godmother. Milly had almost insisted on dressing her, for the present occasion, as one; and it was no fault of the girl's if the good lady had not now appeared in a peaked hat, a short petticoat and diamond shoe-buckles, brandishing the magic crutch. The good lady, in truth, bore herself not less contentedly than if these insignia had marked her work; and Milly's observation to Lord Mark had just been, doubtless, the result of such a light exchange of looks with her as even the great length of the table had not baffled. There were twenty persons between them, but this sustained passage was the sharpest sequel yet to that other comparison of views during the pause on the Swiss pass. It almost appeared to Milly that their fortune had been unduly precipitated—as if, properly, they were in the position of having ventured on a small joke and found the answer out of proportion grave. She could not at this moment, for instance, have said whether, with her quickened perceptions, she were more enlivened or oppressed; and the case might in fact have been serious had she not, by good fortune, from the moment the picture loomed, quickly made up her mind that what finally most concerned her was neither to seek nor to shirk, was not even to wonder too much, but was to let things come as they would, since there was little enough doubt of how they would go.

Everything happened so quickly after that that Milly simply blurted out the first thing that came to mind to the man on her right – who also happened to be sitting on her hostess’s left – saying that she hardly even knew where she was. Those words were her first real acknowledgement of how incredibly romantic the situation felt. They were already at dinner, she and her friend, at Lancaster Gate, surrounded, as it seemed to her, by all things English. Even though she’d only just become aware of Mrs. Lowder’s presence, and even more so of her striking personality, it had all happened so suddenly. Susie, as she often called her friend to be more casual, had only had to wave her hand, and the fairytale began at once. As a result, Susie now glowed, because, with Mrs. Stringham’s newfound success, it really came down to that – in the role of a fairy godmother. Milly had almost insisted on dressing her, for this occasion, as one, and it was only through no fault of Susie’s that the good lady wasn’t currently wearing a pointed hat, a short petticoat, diamond shoe buckles, and waving a magic crutch.

In truth, the good lady seemed as pleased as if she were wearing those things to signify her efforts. Milly’s comment to Lord Mark had surely been prompted by a quick exchange of glances with Susie, a connection that the long table hadn’t managed to interrupt. Even though there were twenty people between them, this subtle conversation was the most obvious result of their earlier discussion while they paused on the Swiss pass.

Milly almost felt as though their fortunes had been rushed, as if they’d made a small joke and received a disproportionately serious response. She couldn’t say at that moment, with her sharper awareness, whether she was more excited or overwhelmed. It might actually have been a serious issue, if she hadn’t, luckily, decided from the moment the situation became clear that the most important thing was neither to seek nor to avoid, not even to overthink it. Instead, she chose to let things unfold as they would, since she had little doubt how they would turn out.

Lord Mark had been brought to her before dinner—not by Mrs. Lowder, but by the handsome girl, that lady's niece, who was now at the other end and on the same side as Susie; he had taken her in, and she meant presently to ask him about Miss Croy, the handsome girl, actually offered to her sight—though now in a splendid way—but for the second time. The first time had been the occasion—only three days before—of her calling at their hotel with her aunt and then making, for our other two heroines, a great impression of beauty and eminence. This impression had remained so with Milly that, at present, and although her attention was aware at the same time of everything else, her eyes were mainly engaged with Kate Croy when not engaged with Susie. That wonderful creature's eyes moreover readily met them—she ranked now as a wonderful creature; and it seemed a part of the swift prosperity of the American visitors that, so little in the original reckoning, she should yet appear conscious, charmingly, frankly conscious, of possibilities of friendship for them. Milly had easily and, as a guest, gracefully generalised: English girls had a special, strong beauty, and it particularly showed in evening dress—above all when, as was strikingly the case with this one, the dress itself was what it should be. That observation she had all ready for Lord Mark when they should, after a little, get round to it. She seemed even now to see that there might be a good deal they would get round to; the indication being that, taken up once for all with her other neighbour, their hostess would leave them much to themselves. Mrs. Lowder's other neighbour was the Bishop of Murrum—a real bishop, such as Milly had never seen, with a complicated costume, a voice like an old-fashioned wind instrument, and a face all the portrait of a prelate; while the gentleman on our young lady's left, a gentleman thick-necked, large and literal, who looked straight before him and as if he were not to be diverted by vain words from that pursuit, clearly counted as an offset to the possession of Lord Mark. As Milly made out these things—with a shade of exhilaration at the way she already fell in—she saw how she was justified of her plea for people and her love of life. It wasn't then, as the prospect seemed to show, so difficult to get into the current, or to stand, at any rate, on the bank. It was easy to get near—if they were near; and yet the elements were different enough from any of her old elements, and positively rich and strange.

"Why shouldn't you have?" Milly asked, observing in this, as she thought, a fine, though, for such a man, a surprisingly artless, fatuity. It was as if there might have been a question of her falsifying for the sake of her own show—that is of her honesty not being proof against her desire to keep well with him herself. She didn't, none the less, otherwise protest against his remark; there was something else she was occupied in seeing. It was the handsome girl alone, one of his own species and his own society, who had made him feel uncertain; of his certainties about a mere little American, a cheap exotic, imported almost wholesale, and whose habitat, with its conditions of climate, growth, and cultivation, its immense profusion, but its few varieties and thin development, he was perfectly satisfied. The marvel was, too, that Milly understood his satisfaction—feeling that she expressed the truth in presently saying: "Of course; I make out that she must be difficult; just as I see that I myself must be easy." And that was what, for all the rest of this occasion, remained with her—as the most interesting thing that could remain. She was more and more content herself to be easy; she would have been resigned, even had it been brought straighter home to her, to passing for a cheap exotic. Provisionally, at any rate, that protected her wish to keep herself, with Lord Mark, in abeyance. They had all affected her as inevitably knowing each other, and if the handsome girl's place among them was something even their initiation couldn't deal with—why, then, she would indeed be a quantity.
