Chapter 14

Chapter Summary & Character Study
Summary

Milly is waiting for a visit from her doctor, Sir Luke Strett, and has decided to allow him free access to her companion, Susan Shepherd.

The narrative focuses on an evening a few days before their departure from London. Milly, Susan, Mrs. Lowder, and Kate Croy are gathered at a hotel after dinner. It is decided that Mrs. Lowder and Susan will go on to a party, while Kate will stay behind with Milly.

After Susan leaves the room to get a shawl and Kate steps out onto a balcony, Mrs. Lowder asks Milly to mention a Mr. Densher to Kate, to find out if he has returned to London. Mrs. Lowder states that she worries he is bothering Kate, who she claims does not care for him. She explains that she cannot ask Kate herself because they never speak of him.

Milly questions this approach, noting that she has never mentioned knowing Mr. Densher to Kate before. They discuss the potential awkwardness of her raising the topic now. Milly asks if Mrs. Lowder likes Mr. Densher, and Mrs. Lowder says she does. Milly confirms that she met him in New York. The conversation continues with Milly raising further difficulties, such as what she should tell Kate about why she is asking. Finally, Milly asks if Mr. Densher has been writing to Kate, which Mrs. Lowder says is what she wants to know.

Just as Milly asks Mrs. Lowder if she believes Mr. Densher is with Kate, Susan returns to the room and Kate reappears in the window. Mrs. Lowder says "Hush!" and then leaves with Susan for the party.

Milly is left with Kate, who remains standing in the window. Milly looks at Kate and, without a word being spoken, understands that Mr. Densher has returned to London.

Character Study

Based on the text, here is a concise character study:

  • *Milly**
    • Milly is initially presented as a woman who has reached a state of grim resolution. Facing a limited number of "hours," she feels "enviably strong" and has a clear plan for an upcoming doctor's visit. She views the sympathy of others with a cynical, self-deprecating humor, labeling herself "gross, greedy, ravenous." Her situation shifts dramatically when Mrs. Lowder confronts her. This external pressure forces her out of her detached state, revealing a deep, hidden vulnerability concerning Mr. Densher. Her composure becomes a performance, requiring her to "smile hard" and carefully choose her words, one of which costs her more than any she has ever spoken. By the end of the scene, her perception has sharpened to the point where she can understand a complex truth simply by observing Kate's posture.
  • *Mrs. Lowder (Aunt Maud)**
    • Mrs. Lowder is presented as a powerful and calculating social strategist. She operates with "rich glibness" and has "fine onyx eyes" that do not blink, signifying her unreadable and determined nature. She initiates the scene's central conflict by attempting to use Milly as a tool to extract information about her niece. She frames her manipulative inquiry as a matter of concern for Kate and Mr. Densher, but her primary motivation is to enforce her own "views." When her initial approach is questioned, she shifts tactics from impatience to a disingenuous show of pity and concern, revealing her skill as a manipulator.
  • *Kate Croy**
    • Kate is presented almost entirely from the outside, as an object of discussion and observation. Initially, she is a silent, enigmatic figure hovering on a balcony. Mrs. Lowder characterizes her as a "proud young woman" who may be secretly defying her. Kate's situation shifts in the final moments as she moves from being the subject of the conversation to its unspoken resolution. Her mere presence, "handsome and upright" with "conscious eyes," is immediately interpreted by Milly as confirmation that Mr. Densher has returned. She communicates this pivotal fact without uttering a single word.
  • *Susan Shepherd (Mrs. Stringham)**
Susan is presented as a benevolent but secondary figure, a companion used by others as a social substitute. Milly is pleased to "put people off with her," and Mrs. Lowder dismisses her as "better than nothing." Her situation is static; she serves primarily as a pawn whose departure and return frame the central, private conversation between Milly and Mrs. Lowder.
