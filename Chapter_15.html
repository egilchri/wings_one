Chapter XV

Chapter Summary & Character Study
Summary

Milly and Kate sit together. While they talk, Kate repeatedly gets up and walks the length of the room. Kate speaks about English society, Aunt Maud, and Lord Mark.

In their conversation, Kate says Aunt Maud is using her as a bargaining chip. Milly insists she is not an obstacle for Kate regarding Lord Mark. Kate advises Milly to leave their social circle. Milly asks why she says such things. Kate replies, "Because you're a dove," then embraces and kisses her.

They hear their companions returning. Mrs. Lowder and Mrs. Stringham enter the room. Kate talks with Mrs. Stringham. Mrs. Lowder asks Milly how the evening went, and Milly replies, "I don't think, dear lady, he's here." Mrs. Lowder calls her an "exquisite thing." The visitors then leave.

The next morning, Milly tells Mrs. Stringham that a doctor, Sir Luke Strett, has an appointment to see her at eleven, but that she will be going out. Milly instructs Mrs. Stringham to tell Sir Luke that Milly is home, and then to meet with him in her place. Milly then says she is going to the National Gallery.

Character Study

Based on the text, here is a concise character study:

  • *Milly Theale** is presented as a passive but highly perceptive observer. She feels herself to be in a "current determined... by others," surrendering to being "dealt with." While physically still, her mind is active, analyzing the performances of those around her. An immediate shift occurs when Kate calls her a "dove." Milly instantly seizes this as a "revealed truth," an identity that gives her a new framework for how to act. This new self-perception prompts a change in her situation: she moves from passively receiving plans to actively, if deceptively, creating one by arranging for Mrs. Stringham to meet Sir Luke Strett in her place.
  • *Kate Croy** is presented as an active and theatrical performer. She is restless, "charming," and in "extraordinary 'form,'" moving and talking to dominate the scene. She projects a hard, cynical frankness, openly deconstructing her aunt, English society, and her own "commercial" position. Her situation shifts abruptly in response to Milly's simple, emotional question. She pivots from a pacing, almost predatory ("panther") cynicism to a ceremonial tenderness, bestowing the "dove" identity upon Milly with a kiss, thus changing the dynamic between them from a lecture to an anointing.
  • *Mrs. Lowder (Aunt Maud)** is presented as a powerful social strategist whose influence is felt even when she is absent. Kate describes her as a manipulator who uses her niece as a commodity. When she appears, her purpose is to regain control of the situation ("catch hold... of the loose end"). Her immediate situation shifts when Milly directly counters her unspoken plan; Mrs. Lowder's response is a look of "deep criticism" followed by effusive, tactical praise ("Oh, you exquisite thing!"), showing her ability to adapt instantly to a setback.
  • *Mrs. Stringham (Susie)** is presented as an imaginative and loyal companion. Her role is supportive, and she is easily enrolled in Milly’s plans. Upon hearing Milly’s new arrangement for the doctor, her initial shock is quickly "engulfed" by an "extravagance of assent," immediately accepting her assigned part in the "Byzantine" scheme.

It was doubtless because this queer form of directness had in itself, for the hour, seemed so sufficient that Milly was afterwards aware of having really, all the while—during the strange, indescribable session before the return of their companions—done nothing to intensify it. If she was most aware only afterwards, under the long, discurtained ordeal of the morrow's dawn, that was because she had really, till their evening's end came, ceased, after a little, to miss anything from their ostensible comfort. What was behind showed but in gleams and glimpses; what was in front never at all confessed to not holding the stage. Three minutes had not passed before Milly quite knew she should have done nothing Aunt Maud had just asked her. She knew it moreover by much the same light that had acted for her with that lady and with Sir Luke Strett. It pressed upon her then and there that she was still in a current determined, through her indifference, timidity, bravery, generosity—she scarce could say which—by others; that not she but the current acted, and that somebody else, always, was the keeper of the lock or the dam. Kate for example had but to open the flood-gate: the current moved in its mass—the current, as it had been, of her doing as Kate wanted. What, somehow, in the most extraordinary way in the world, had Kate wanted but to be, of a sudden, more interesting than she had ever been? Milly, for their evening then, quite held her breath with the appreciation of it. If she hadn't been sure her companion would have had nothing, from her moments with Mrs. Lowder, to go by, she would almost have seen the admirable creature "cutting in" to anticipate a danger. This fantasy indeed, while they sat together, dropped after a little; even if only because other fantasies multiplied and clustered, making fairly, for our young woman, the buoyant medium in which her friend talked and moved. They sat together, I say, but Kate moved as much as she talked; she figured there, restless and charming, just perhaps a shade perfunctory, repeatedly quitting her place, taking slowly, to and fro, in the trailing folds of her light dress, the length of the room, and almost avowedly performing for the pleasure of her hostess.

Mrs. Lowder had told Milly at Matcham that she and her niece, working together, could practically rule the world. While that statement had a vague, romantic appeal back then, Milly now understood it more clearly. Kate, on her own, could probably achieve anything. Milly, on the other hand, was probably only concerned with the part of the world that directly affected her, the part she needed to deal with first. Based on this understanding, she figured she'd contribute her share to their "conquest." She would provide something, and Kate would take something – both of them fulfilling Aunt Maud's expectations.

Essentially, in the quiet lamplight, it felt like a practice run for a major event. Milly felt she was being "dealt with" graciously and completely. She accepted it because she knew she was contributing her positive energy. And Kate took what Milly offered just as freely and gratefully, renewing the bond between them with each of their long, slow walks, validating her companion's contribution simply by showing interest in it. Of course, Milly assumed the interest was mainly for herself; Kate's interest was probably secondary.

All this talk, this quick hour before they stopped, stemmed from the fact that the beautiful Kate was in top form. Milly remembered Kate saying she was best late at night, and how that statement had made her wonder when *she* was at her best, and how lucky people were to have a consistent time like that. She had no consistent time; she was never at her best – unless, of course, she was listening, watching, admiring, and yielding, like now.

Moreover, if Kate had never been so impressive, the amazing thing was that she had never been so open. Kate, being the type of person she was, could open up, even while guiding and advising Milly. She could be ironic, confidential, and expressive, and tell things she never told before. That was the feeling: that she was finally revealing things, maybe for her own benefit as well. It was as if Milly's errors in perception and understanding, and her remaining naivety, were occasionally too much for Kate to handle.

Kate addressed those irritations with an amused energy that Milly might have found cynical, but that was necessary, as the other woman saw it, because of the way the American mind processed things in certain social situations. It seemed to Kate – who was observing an enthralled and amazed Milly – that the American mind couldn't grasp English society without analyzing every single case. It couldn't use a certain method – Milly didn’t know the exact term, until she suggested analogy and induction, and then instinct, none of which were quite right. Instead, the American mind had to be guided and introduced to each aspect of the "monster" (English society), allowed to explore it thoroughly, whether for extreme delight or, as Kate saw it, for excessive shock.

This "monster," Kate admitted, might seem huge to those who were used to less complex forms of society, and therefore, undoubtedly, less entertaining. It might be a strange and terrifying monster, designed to hurt the naive, humble the proud, and offend the virtuous. But if you had to deal with it, you had to learn how, instead of being constantly on edge. And that’s what Kate was, in essence, showing Milly tonight.

She gave away publicly, in this process, Lancaster Gate and everything it contained; she gave away, hand over hand, Milly's thrill continued to note, Aunt Maud and Aunt Maud's glories and Aunt Maud's complacencies; she gave herself away most of all, and it was naturally what most contributed to her candour. She didn't speak to her friend once more, in Aunt Maud's strain, of how they could scale the skies; she spoke, by her bright, perverse preference on this occasion, of the need, in the first place, of being neither stupid nor vulgar. It might have been a lesson, for our young American, in the art of seeing things as they were—a lesson so various and so sustained that the pupil had, as we have shown, but receptively to gape. The odd thing furthermore was that it could serve its purpose while explicitly disavowing every personal bias. It wasn't that she disliked Aunt Maud, who was everything she had on other occasions declared; but the dear woman, ineffaceably stamped by inscrutable nature and a dreadful art, wasn't—how could she be?—what she wasn't. She wasn't any one. She wasn't anything. She wasn't anywhere. Milly mustn't think it—one couldn't, as a good friend, let her. Those hours at Matcham were inespérées, were pure manna from heaven; or if not wholly that perhaps, with humbugging old Lord Mark as a backer, were vain as a ground for hopes and calculations. Lord Mark was very well, but he wasn't the cleverest creature in England, and even if he had been he still wouldn't have been the most obliging. He weighed it out in ounces, and indeed each of the pair was really waiting for what the other would put down.
