Chapter 2

Chapter Summary & Character Study
Summary

After her mother’s death, a young woman went to live with her wealthy aunt, Mrs. Lowder (Aunt Maud). While living in her aunt’s house, she discovered she enjoyed material comforts. She also discovered that her aunt had long been concerned about her family. The young woman, Kate, spent much of her time alone in her room, feeling pressure from her aunt and postponing a "surrender" to her.

Kate visited her sister, Marian, a widow with four children. During the visit, which took place in Marian's home after the children’s dinner, Marian told Kate not to "throw herself away." Marian explained that she was afraid Kate would marry Merton Densher, whom she dislikes for having no money. Marian insisted that Kate must marry for money and do what their Aunt Maud wants.

Kate replied that their father had told her the same thing the previous day. She then revealed that she had visited their father and offered to go live with him. Marian reacted with horror. Kate explained that their father had refused her offer, believing she should stay with Aunt Maud and threatening to curse her if she left.

As Kate was leaving, she asked whom Marian meant by "Aunt Maud's young man." Marian named Lord Mark. Kate dismissed this as gossip. To end the conversation, they agreed that Marian would stop mentioning Lord Mark as long as Kate assured her she was not involved with anyone else. Kate denied everything and left.

Character Study

Based on the text, here is a concise character study:

  • *Kate Croy**
    • Kate is presented as an intelligent, observant, and deeply conflicted young woman whose situation has shifted dramatically. Forced by her mother's death and financial ruin to live with her wealthy aunt, she is acutely aware of her new dependence. She is introspective, analyzing her own surprising susceptibility to the material comforts her aunt provides, while also feeling trapped and scrutinized. She views herself as a "trembling kid" before the "lioness" Aunt Maud, and feels a burdensome, resentful duty toward her sister. She hides her true thoughts from others, often accepting their interpretations of her actions to maintain peace, a state she sees as a "hypocrisy of stupidity."
  • *Mrs. Lowder (Aunt Maud)**
    • Presented entirely through Kate's perception, Aunt Maud is a formidable and powerful figure. She is physically described as "majestic" and "magnificent," but her personality is seen as "sharp," controlling, and possessing a "strong will." Kate views her as a "lioness" and a "besieger," a complex and "unscrupulous" force who is both "passionate" and "practical." In the past, she maintained a calculated distance from Kate's family, offering just enough to create a sense of grievance. Now, having taken Kate in, her influence is an immense, looming pressure that dictates Kate's options.
  • *Marian Condrip**
Kate’s elder sister, Marian, is presented as a woman diminished by circumstance. A "demoralised, querulous" widow with children, she has lost her youthful charm and become, in Kate’s eyes, selfish and manipulative. Her situation is one of financial desperation, and she relentlessly pressures Kate to secure their aunt’s fortune for the family’s benefit. She is fixated on Kate making a strategic marriage and is openly hostile to any prospect, like Merton Densher, who lacks money. Her perspective is portrayed as vulgar and "abject," shaped by her unhappy marriage and the influence of her late husband's family.

She had gone to Mrs. Lowder on her mother's death—gone with an effort the strain and pain of which made her at present, as she recalled them, reflect on the long way she had travelled since then. There had been nothing else to do—not a penny in the other house, nothing but unpaid bills that had gathered thick while its mistress lay mortally ill, and the admonition that there was nothing she must attempt to raise money on, since everything belonged to the "estate." How the estate would turn out at best presented itself as a mystery altogether gruesome; it had proved, in fact, since then a residuum a trifle less scant than, with Marian, she had for some weeks feared; but the girl had had at the beginning rather a wounded sense of its being watched on behalf of Marian and her children. What on earth was it supposed that she wanted to do to it? She wanted in truth only to give up—to abandon her own interest, which she, no doubt, would already have done had not the point been subject to Aunt Maud's sharp intervention. Aunt Maud's intervention was all sharp now, and the other point, the great one, was that it was to be, in this light, either all put up with or all declined. Yet at the winter's end, nevertheless, she could scarce have said what stand she conceived she had taken. It wouldn't be the first time she had seen herself obliged to accept with smothered irony other people's interpretation of her conduct.
