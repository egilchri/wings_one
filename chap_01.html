Chapter I

Chapter Summary & Character Study
Summary

Kate Croy waits in her father's shabby lodgings. While waiting, she moves between the sofa and an armchair, looks at the prints on the wall, and stands on the balcony. She also looks at her reflection in a mirror over the mantel, readjusting her hat and hair.

Her father, Lionel Croy, eventually enters. He says he has been to the chemist because he is unwell. Kate tells him he looks well and then says she is willing to go away and live with him. He expresses surprise and refuses her offer.

Kate explains that her Aunt Maud has made her a proposal with one condition: that Kate must break off all contact with her father. In response, Kate has come to him. Mr. Croy says this is a proper requirement from her aunt and advises Kate to accept the offer. He tells her she should use him to get a better deal from her aunt, suggesting she make her aunt feel how much of a sacrifice it is to give him up. He advises her to marry a man her aunt selects, after which he will resume relations with her.

Kate again offers to choose him over her aunt and asks him to stand with her. He refuses, calls her sick, and accuses her of being in love with someone her aunt disapproves of. He opens the door for her to leave. He asks who the man is, and Kate replies that the man wants to be kind to him. Mr. Croy forbids her from marrying anyone her aunt disapproves of and threatens to make her regret it if she defies them. Kate says she is not afraid of him.

He gives her his final advice and reveals his anger that she gives half of her inheritance to her sister, Marian, instead of to him.

Character Study

Based on the text, here is a concise character study.

### Kate Croy

Kate is introduced in a state of controlled impatience and disgust with her surroundings, which she sees as a direct reflection of her family’s “failure of fortune and of honour.” She is presented as proud, self-aware, and resilient; while observing the shabby room, she also observes her own handsome reflection in the mirror, affirming her personal value as separate from her family's collapse.

Her situation shifts due to an ultimatum from her Aunt Maud: she will be taken care of financially if she completely severs ties with her father. In response, Kate makes a decisive move, coming to her father not to abandon him but to offer to live with him and reject her aunt’s proposal. When he refuses, deducing she is in love with someone her aunt disapproves of, her situation shifts again. She is left isolated, rejected by the father she offered to save, and must now proceed alone.

### Lionel Croy (The Father)

Mr. Croy is presented through his absence and deception before he even appears. He has lied about being ill and inhabits a "vulgar" space that belies his appearance. When he enters, he is a man of profound contradictions: physically immaculate, charming, and looking the part of a "perfectly" respectable English gentleman, yet he is entirely untrustworthy, manipulative, and amoral. He "dealt out lies" and his polished exterior serves to mask his inner corruption.

His situation is immediately challenged by Kate’s unexpected offer. His initial plan was to make a grand, noble show of sacrificing her for her own good. When she preempts this, he is momentarily "disconcerted" but quickly pivots. His shift is from feigned parental concern to that of a cynical strategist, advising Kate to leverage her "duty" to him to get a better financial deal from her aunt. He ultimately rejects Kate’s genuine appeal for solidarity, revealing his core motivation is not parental feeling but resentment that she gave her inheritance money to her sister instead of to him.
