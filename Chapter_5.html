Chapter V

Chapter Summary & Character Study
Summary

Two ladies, Mrs. Stringham and Milly Theale, travel together through the Brünig pass in Switzerland. They had previously met in Boston, after which Mrs. Stringham visited Milly in New York. Following the visit, Milly sent a telegram proposing an immediate, unconditional trip to Europe, which Mrs. Stringham accepted. They traveled by sea to Naples and then made their way through Italy and over the Alps.

While on the Brünig, they stop for lunch and agree to prolong their stay. Milly leaves Mrs. Stringham to make the arrangements for lodging and their carriage. Milly then takes a book and strolls off. After an hour, Mrs. Stringham goes to find her. She follows a path up a hillside and past some chalets, where an old woman indicates the direction Milly took. Mrs. Stringham continues on and finds Milly's book lying on a rock.

From her position, she sees Milly sitting on a rock slab at the edge of a precipice. Mrs. Stringham watches her for several minutes without revealing her presence. She then quietly withdraws. After waiting for some time, she returns to the path where the book is, writes "à bientôt!" on its cover with a pencil, and goes back to the inn. Late in the afternoon, Milly reappears.

Character Study

Based on the text, here is a concise character study.

### Mrs. Susan Stringham

  • **Initial Presentation:** Mrs. Stringham is a middle-aged, childless New England widow and a writer of modest reputation. She presents herself as a "woman of the world" due to a European education in her youth, and she leads a structured, intellectually-inclined life in Boston. She is self-aware, observant, and prone to "shy conceits," living a life more of refined observation than of direct, passionate experience.
  • **Immediate Shift:** Her situation shifts dramatically upon meeting Milly Theale. Her quiet, ordered life is completely upended. She renounces her own ambitions to become Milly’s devoted companion, shifting her role from an independent woman to a self-appointed "confidant." This new relationship forces her into a state of constant, "secretive" observation and analysis, which she recognizes is contrary to her nature. Her perception culminates in a moment of terror on the mountain, which then resolves into a "revelation": she no longer sees Milly as merely restless but as a grand figure confronting a profound and complex destiny.

    • ### Milly Theale

  • **Initial Presentation:** Milly is presented almost exclusively through Mrs. Stringham’s admiring and bewildered eyes. She is a young, strikingly unconventional New Yorker, described as "slim, pale, delicately haggard" with exceptional red hair and dressed in mourning. She is an orphan of immense wealth who has suffered numerous bereavements. Characterized by a "charming, ambiguous oddity," she is at once restlessly impatient, inexplicably sad, and charmingly gay. To Mrs. Stringham, she is a "princess" who seems tragically inexperienced and "starved for culture."
  • **Immediate Shift:** The narrative begins with Milly successfully undertaking a European tour against advice. Her situation, however, shifts from that of a wealthy, melancholy tourist to something far more serious. Her solitary contemplation on a dangerous cliff edge transforms her from a figure of romantic restlessness into one of immense gravity. The ambiguity surrounding her "unpacified state" sharpens into the impression that she is consciously facing "the whole assault of life," and is reserved for a "complicated passage" rather than a simple resolution.

It was the judgment of the head-waiters and other functionaries on the Italian lakes that approved itself now as interested; they themselves had been conscious of impatiences, of bolder dreams—at least the younger had; so that one of the things they made out together—making out as they did an endless variety—was that in those operatic palaces of the Villa d'Este, of Cadenabbia, of Pallanza and Stresa, lone women, however reinforced by a travelling-library of instructive volumes, were apt to be beguiled and undone. She was looking down on all the world, and while that in itself could be overwhelming, she wasn't considering renouncing it. Was she choosing among the kingdoms of the earth, or did she want them all? This question, before Mrs. Stringham had decided what to do, made all others irrelevant. So she realized, or thought she did, that while it might be dangerous to call out, to in any way startle her, it would probably be safe enough to leave as she had come. She watched a little longer, held her breath, and never afterward knew how much time had passed.

Not many minutes probably, yet they had not seemed few, and they had given her so much to think of, not only while creeping home, but while waiting afterwards at the inn, that she was still busy with them when, late in the afternoon, Milly reappeared. She had stopped at the point of the path where the Tauchnitz lay, had taken it up and, with the pencil attached to her watch-guard, had scrawled a word—à bientôt!—across the cover; then, even under the girl's continued delay, had measured time without a return of alarm. For she now saw that the great thing she had brought away was precisely a conviction that the future was not to exist for her princess in the form of any sharp or simple release from the human predicament. It wouldn't be for her a question of a flying leap and thereby of a quick escape. It would be a question of taking full in the face the whole assault of life, to the general muster of which indeed her face might have been directly presented as she sat there on her rock. Mrs. Stringham was thus able to say to herself, even after another interval of some length, that if her young friend still continued absent it wouldn't be because—whatever the opportunity—she had cut short the thread. She wouldn't have committed suicide; she knew herself unmistakably reserved for some more complicated passage; this was the very vision in which she had, with no little awe, been discovered. The image that thus remained with the elder lady kept the character of revelation. During the breathless minutes of her watch she had seen her companion afresh; the latter's type, aspect, marks, her history, her state, her beauty, her mystery, all unconsciously betrayed themselves to the Alpine air, and all had been gathered in again to feed Mrs. Stringham's flame. They are things that will more distinctly appear for us, and they are meanwhile briefly represented by the enthusiasm that was stronger on our friend's part than any doubt. It was a consciousness she was scarce yet used to carrying, but she had as beneath her feet a mine of something precious. She seemed to herself to stand near the mouth, not yet quite cleared. The mine but needed working and would certainly yield a treasure. She was not thinking, either, of Milly's gold.
