Chapter IV

Chapter Summary & Character Study
Summary

A man receives a telegram from Mrs. Lowder and goes to her house at Lancaster Gate, where he waits in her drawing-room for a long time. When Mrs. Lowder appears, she tells him that she intends for her niece, Kate, to marry a "great man." She says that while he is incompatible with this plan, she will not banish him but will allow him to continue to see Kate.

The man, Densher, later tells Kate about the meeting. He also informs her that his newspaper has offered him a multi-week assignment in America, which he must start immediately. Kate urges him to go. He then tells her about his foreign upbringing, which Mrs. Lowder had criticized.

While in a park, Kate spontaneously tells Densher, "I engage myself to you for ever," and he pledges himself to her in return. They move to a more private spot where they exchange vows and seal their compact, becoming an affianced couple. They agree to keep their engagement a secret for the time being. They discuss how they will correspond while he is away and what Kate will say if her aunt questions their relationship. Kate says she will admit that she loves him. Densher embraces her before he departs.

Based on the text, here is a concise character study:

  • *Merton Densher**
    • He is presented as a highly analytical and self-aware man, conscious of his social and financial inferiority. He enters the situation prepared to be "patient, ridiculous, [and] diplomatic" for the woman he loves. Initially, he is intimidated by Mrs. Lowder's wealth and power, which he views with aesthetic revulsion, feeling it as a "portentous negation of his own world." His situation shifts dramatically when the dreaded confrontation with Mrs. Lowder turns into a surprisingly civil, strategic negotiation. This is immediately followed by an unexpected professional opportunity—a journalistic assignment in America—and a secret engagement to Kate. He moves from a position of anxious waiting to one of active commitment and impending departure.
  • *Mrs. Lowder (Aunt Maud)**
    • She is presented as a formidable, powerful, and strategic figure, whose character is first established by her "massive, florid" and "colossally vulgar" home. Densher perceives her as a "lioness" and a force comparable to the "car of Juggernaut." In person, however, she is surprisingly "bland" and "adroit," choosing not to use open aggression. She sees her niece as a valuable "investment" and operates with a clear, long-term plan. Her situation remains unchanged; she is the established power, confidently manipulating events by granting limited approval to Densher, whom she shrewdly assesses and seems to personally, if conditionally, like.
  • *Kate Croy**
Presented through Densher's eyes as a figure of "pride," she reveals herself in dialogue to be intelligent, pragmatic, and decisive. She fully understands her aunt's strategy and immediately grasps the importance of Densher's job offer, urging him to accept. Her situation shifts from being the subject of a power struggle to an active agent in her own future. She initiates a secret but profound engagement with Densher ("I engage myself to you for ever"), solidifying their bond and establishing a clear, clandestine plan before his departure.
